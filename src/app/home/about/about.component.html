EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Status Capital Building Society is an Eswatini registered and licensed financial services provider carrying on the business of a building society within the parameters of the Building Societies Act of 1962. Our head office and main branch is based at The Crescent, Ezulwini.

Although the Status Capital Building Society was ﬁrst registered in 2019, it can draw on the vast skills and industry expertise of its assembled staff, management and business partners. Status Capital Building Society has developed its Vision and Mission in line with the National Development Strategy from the Minister of Economic Planning and Development and focus on SME Lending, Vendor Financing, Agriculture and Farming.

The “Making Access Possible” (MAP) study (2014) on financial in Eswatini developed jointly by UNCDF, FinMark Trust (FMT) and the Centre for Financial Regulation and Inclusion (Cenfri) on financial inclusion revealed that only 3.7% of self-employed people had access to formal credit. This means that 96.3% of SME’s in the country are excluded from accessing credit. This harms the economy and Status Capital Building Society will be seeking to address and redress that by providing vendor financing and improving financial inclusion to boost SME’s in Eswatini. MAP noted that;

“Access to credit is important for most businesses wanting to grow, including SME’s which frequently require large capital outlay to finance start-up, input and asset costs relative to small amounts of savings.”

Status Capital Building Society will, therefore, be playing our role to ensure that we improve financial inclusion in the SME sector in line with the country’s commitment towards sustainable development goals (SDG’s). We commit to economic development as well as economic growth.

According to MAP (2014):

“The irregular nature of farming incomes and the high capital investment required upfront in terms of seed, fertiliser or livestock, and farming equipment means that many farmers require credit upfront to fund this initial capital investment.”

Assisting small scale and other start-up farmers with access to finance is very important for the country’s economy and for improving food security and food production in the country.

THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN A BANK AND A BUILDING SOCIETY

Few people would say they understand the difference between a bank and a building society. So, how do banks and building societies stack up against one another?
We will examine the differences as well as which is safer for your hard-earned money in the following sections. Is it safer to deposit your money in a traditional bank or a building society?
Banks and building societies have traditionally been intrinsically linked in terms of core principles and how they are run. But what are the true distinctions between the two?

What is a Bank

A bank is a financial institution licenced to receive deposits and provide loans (but not payday loans). Commercial banks are concerned with managing withdrawals and receiving deposits in addition to supplying short-term loans to individual customers and businesses.

What is a Building Society

A building society is a financial institution that offers higher interest on savings and investments, to its members. It also offers mortgages and provides banking and other financial services to its members.

Basic Differences

Banks are companies that are usually listed on the stock market, hence they are owned by and run for the benefit of their own shareholders.

Building societies on the flip side do not have external shareholders involved in their business.

Investors and deposit holders become “members” of the society. These members can vote on decisions that will affect the overall building society. Since building societies are not required to pay dividends to any shareholders due to the lack of them, it means that they can consequently offer better interest rates on savings and mortgages to their members.

How Building Societies are run

There appears to be a lot of confusion about what distinguishes banks and building societies in terms of how they are run.

The common misconception is that building societies rely entirely on customer savings deposits, but this is not the case.
Building societies receive funding not only from savings deposits but also from retail and institutional funds such as pension funds; however, building societies receive this funding in the form of fixed deposits or membership subscriptions rather than shareholder investments.

FUTURE-PROOFING OUR BUSINESS STRATEGY

On the back of demutualization and legal changes introduced to the sector in the 1980’s (in the UK specifically), the competitive playing field is broadening, necessitating the need for building societies to evolve their offering away from the traditional model and environment within which they operate. A move that is essential to their very survival.

Key challenges facing building societies

Increasing competitive intensity

  • Challenger banks encroaching on their territory
  • New online-only banks challenging in specific segments – low operating costs and highly intuitive engagement models
  • High-street banks challenging in the mortgage space – leveraging their scale and funding structures

Tougher market conditions

  • Ongoing low interest rates – no evidence of immediate up turn
  • Negative effect on traditional profit- generation strategies
  • Property sector most vulnerable due to political uncertainty and fluctuations in the global economy
  • Regulatory intervention adding cost layers to compliance for retail banking product providers and building societies

    • Evolving customer needs and behaviour

  • Consumers of all ages now expect integrated omnichannel experience from financial services providers
  • Building societies lag other sectors in their digital offerings
  • Increasing life expectancy necessitates evolution of services and products – especially wealth management services and sustainable pension provision products
