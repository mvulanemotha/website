Access to finance for SMMEs remains a formidable obstacle in Eswatini. SCBS has developed its Vision and Mission in line with the National Development Strategy from the Minister of Economic Planning and Development and focus on Agriculture and Farming, SMME Lending and Vendor Financing.

The "Making Access Possible" (MAP) study (2014) on financial in Eswatini developed jointly by UNCDF, FinMark Trust (FTM) and the Center for Financial Regulation and include (Cenfri) on financial inclusion revealed that only 3.7 % of self-employed people had access to formal credit. This means that 96.3 % of SMME's in the country are excluded from accessing credit. This has a negative impact in the economy and SCBS will be seeking to address and redress that by providing vendor financing and improving financial inclusion to boost SMME's in Eswatini. MAP noted that:
"Access to credit is important for most businesses waiting to grow, including SMME's which frequantly equire large capital outlay to finance start-up, input and asset costs relative to small amounts of savings"


SCBS will therefore play its role in ensuring that the Society improves financial inclusion in the SMME sector in line with the country's commitment towards substainable development goals (SDG's) and makes a commitment towards contributing to the country's economic growth and development.

The Revised Small, Micro & Medium Enterprise Policy (2018), of Eswatini from the Ministry of Commerce, Industry and Trade encourages all stakeholders, including Building Societies to support the development of the SMME Sector. The policy encourages building societies and mortgage finance providers to provide loans to SMMEs desirous of purchasing land or property for business use. Although SCBS has not expanded to the extent of providing Mortgage to SMMEs, the Society has introduced Purchase Order Financing to SMMEs that do not have access to the funding they need to fulfill their large purchase order needs.



What is the diffence between a bank and building Society


Banks and Building Societies have been intrinsically linked in terms of core principles and how they are run a few people would say they understand the difference between the two. But what are the two distinctions between the two?

Basic Differences

Banks are companies that are usually listed on the stock market, hence they are owned by and run for the benefit of thier own shareholders.

Building societies on the flip side do not have external shareholders involved in thier business.

Investors and deposit holders become "members" of the society. These members can vote on decisions that will affect the overall building society. Since building societies are not required to pay dividends to any shareholders due to the lack of them, it means that they can consequently offer better interest rates on savings and mortgages to their members.


How Building Societies are run

There appears to be a lot of confusion about what distinguishes banks and building societies in terms of how they are run.

The common misconception is that building societies rely entirely on customer savings deposits, but this is not the case. Building societies receive funding not only from savings deposits but also from retail and institutional funds such as pension funds, however, building societies receive this funding in the form of fixed deposits or membership subscriptions rather that shareholder investment.

Key challenges facing building societies

Increasing competitive intensity

  • Challenger banks encroaching on their territory
  • New online-only banks challenging in specific segments – low operating costs and highly intuitive engagement models
  • High-street banks challenging in the mortgage space – leveraging their scale and funding structures
Tougher market conditions

  • Ongoing low interest rates – no evidence of immediate up turn
  • Negative effect on traditional profit- generation strategies
  • Property sector most vulnerable due to political uncertainty and fluctuations in the global economy
  • Regulatory intervention adding cost layers to compliance for retail banking product providers and building societies
Evolving customer needs and behaviour

  • Consumers of all ages now expect integrated omnichannel experience from financial services providers
  • Building societies lag other sectors in their digital offerings
  • Increasing life expectancy necessitates evolution of services and products – especially wealth management services and sustainable pension provision products
